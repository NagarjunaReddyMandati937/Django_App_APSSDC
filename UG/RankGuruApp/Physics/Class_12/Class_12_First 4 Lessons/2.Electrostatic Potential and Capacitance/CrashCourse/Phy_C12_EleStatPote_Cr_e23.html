We resolve the electric dipole into horizontal and vertical components as shown in the figure.


    Parallel component of electric field

    \[ E_{\parallel} = \dfrac{2p \cos\theta}{4\pi \epsilon_o r^3} \]


    Perpendicular of electric field

    \[ E_{\perp} = \dfrac{p\sin \theta}{4\pi \epsilon_o r^3} \]


    Potential due to dipole

    \[ V = -\int \vec{E_{\parallel}} .\vec{dr} - \int \vec{E_{\perp}}. \vec{dr} \]


    Second term goes to zero as

    \[ \vec{E_{\perp}} \]

    is perpendicular to

    \[ \vec{dr} \]

    .


    Thus potential due to dipole

    \[ V = -\dfrac{2p \cos\theta}{4\pi \epsilon_o } \int \dfrac{dr}{r^3} \]


    Or

    \[ V = -\dfrac{2p \cos\theta}{4\pi \epsilon_o } \times \dfrac{1}{(-2) r^2} \]


    \[ \implies V = \dfrac{p \cos\theta}{4\pi \epsilon_o r^2} \]
